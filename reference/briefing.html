1. Executive summary — plain English

Ask a modern language model the same forecasting question two ways — for example, “Will the Fed cut rates before year-end?” versus “Before December 31, is a Fed rate cut likely?” — and you will get two different probabilities. Not one probability for the underlying world event, but two numbers attached to two surface forms of the same question. That is the failure mode this paper addresses. We call it frame-variance: the forecaster’s output depends on the wording, not just the world.

Frame-variance is not a cosmetic issue. Recent audits show that the spread between paraphrases can exceed 15 percentage points on frontier models (Paleka et al. 2024), and it is large enough that one can construct a Dutch book against a frame-variant forecaster — a portfolio of bets that guarantees profit off the inconsistency, with no opinion about the world required. A market that can be Dutch-booked is broken; so is a forecaster.

Existing responses all come after the fact: measure the inconsistency, or patch the outputs by projecting them onto the nearest “coherent” set of probabilities. Neither trains a model to be frame-invariant in the first place. Our contribution is a reinforcement-learning recipe that does. The reward has two parts — one rewards getting the probability right on each paraphrase (standard Brier), the other penalizes disagreement across paraphrases of the same question. A single knob $\lambda \geq 0$ controls the trade-off between accuracy and invariance.

Theoretically, the objective has the property we want: in the limit of infinite invariance weight and unlimited model capacity, its unique maximizer is exactly the Bayes-optimal forecaster that gives the same probability to every paraphrase — that is, the forecaster a rational agent with full information would produce. Empirically, we train a 7B-parameter open-weights model on roughly 3,000 resolved prediction-market questions, paraphrase each one eight ways, and compare against five strong baselines (zero-shot, supervised fine-tuning, RL without invariance, RoParQ-style fine-tuning, and post-hoc coherence projection).

Three outcomes are possible and all are publishable: invariance helps (accuracy improves or is preserved while wording-dependence drops), invariance is free (accuracy unchanged, wording-dependence drops — still a useful finding for agentic forecasting systems), or invariance hurts (accuracy degrades). The first two would be the core result; the third would be a clean negative result about the cost of robustness, which the workshop explicitly welcomes.

If you remember one thing: the method trains a language model to give the same probability to logically equivalent paraphrases of a forecasting question, using a single reinforcement-learning objective with a provable optimality guarantee, and tests whether that invariance transfers to paraphrases written by a held-out paraphraser it has never seen.

2. The problem: frame-variance in LLM forecasters

2.1 What the literature has shown

Three pieces of evidence establish that frame-variance is a real, measurable failure mode, not a curiosity:

  • Prompt-format sensitivity. Sclar et al. (2024) document up to 76-point accuracy swings on reasoning benchmarks from prompt-format changes that preserve meaning.
  • Arbitrage against frontier forecasters. Paleka et al. (2024) construct explicit Dutch books against frontier LLMs on logically-equivalent forecasting questions: if you take the model’s probability on paraphrase A and bet against the model’s probability on paraphrase B (same underlying event), you can lock in a guaranteed profit.
  • Answer instability on QA. RoParQ (Kahn et al. 2025) introduces XParaCon, the standard deviation of answer correctness across paraphrases, and finds it is nontrivially large even on 70B-class models.

2.2 Why post-hoc patches aren’t enough

Two classes of fixes exist:

  1. Audit / measure. Report how incoherent the model is (Paleka 2024, Kendiukhov 2025, Zhu & Griffiths 2024). Useful as diagnostic but does not make the model better.
  2. Post-hoc projection. Take the incoherent outputs and project them onto the nearest coherent set — the coherence polytope (Predd 2009, Osherson & Vardi 2006, Irving 2025). This is a patch, not a fix: the model’s internal representation is unchanged, and whether projection generalizes to new paraphrase distributions depends entirely on how close those distributions are to what was projected.

Training-time inducement is different. It changes the policy itself. If the invariance transfers to held-out paraphrase distributions — a claim we test directly — it is evidence that the model has learned something about semantic equivalence, not merely compressed a particular surface form.

5. The RL training function

5.1 Setup

A forecasting question is a pair $(Q, Y)$ with question text $Q$ and binary outcome $Y \in \{0, 1\}$. A paraphrase generator $\rho: Q \mapsto \Delta(\mathcal X)$ produces text paraphrases. At each training step we draw $K$ paraphrases $x_1, \dots, x_K \sim \rho(\cdot \mid Q)$ of the same seed question. The policy $\pi_\theta$ — a language model — emits a chain-of-thought followed by a numeric probability $p_i = \pi_\theta(x_i) \in [0,1]$ on each paraphrase.

5.2 Per-sample reward

$$ \boxed{\; R_i \;=\; -\underbrace{(p_i - Y)^2}_{\text{per-paraphrase Brier}} \;-\; \lambda \cdot \underbrace{(p_i - \bar{p})^2}_{\text{deviation from consensus}} \;} $$

Here $\bar p = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{j=1}^K p_j$ (computed under stop-gradient, so the variance term pulls each $p_i$ toward a fixed target rather than creating a self-referential fixed point), and $\lambda \geq 0$ is the invariance weight.

5.3 Aggregate form (bias-variance identity)

Set $V = \tfrac{1}{K}\sum_i (p_i - \bar p)^2$ and $B_{\text{cons}} = (\bar p - Y)^2$. Then the mean per-sample reward has a closed form:

$$ \boxed{\;\frac{1}{K}\sum_i R_i \;=\; -\,B_{\text{cons}} \;-\; (1 + \lambda)\,V\;} $$

Two things to notice. First, vanilla per-sample Brier already penalizes paraphrase variance with weight $1$ — a consequence of the bias-variance identity. Second, our $\lambda$ is an extra penalty on top of that, so the effective variance weight in the objective is $1 + \lambda$. Setting $\lambda = 0$ exactly recovers standard per-sample Brier (Turtel et al. 2025 corresponds to this). Setting $\lambda \to \infty$ forces invariance.

5.4 GRPO full loss with KL anchor

We use per-sample rewards $R_i$ (rather than a single group-level reward) because per-sample advantages give denser gradient signal. The advantages are computed within-group:

$$ A_i \;=\; \frac{R_i - \operatorname{mean}_j R_j}{\operatorname{std}_j R_j + \varepsilon} $$

The GRPO loss with KL anchor to the reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$:

$$ \mathcal L(\theta) \;=\; -\mathbb E_{(Q,Y) \sim \mathcal D}\;\mathbb E_{x_{1:K} \sim \rho(\cdot \mid Q)} \!\left[\, \sum_{i=1}^K A_i \, \log \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x_i) \,\right] \;+\; \beta \cdot \operatorname{KL}\!\bigl(\pi_\theta \,\Vert\, \pi_{\text{ref}}\bigr) $$

The KL term is standard RLHF / GRPO. It prevents the policy from drifting too far from the base model — which would otherwise be one way for the objective to “achieve invariance” (collapse to a constant). With $\beta > 0$ the policy must stay close to the pretrained prior, and the only way to reduce variance across paraphrases is to make the paraphrases actually look the same to the model.

6. Proofs

6.1 Bias-variance identity

Claim. For any $p_1,\dots,p_K \in \mathbb R$ and $Y \in \mathbb R$,

$$ \underbrace{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^K (p_i - Y)^2}_{B_{\text{mean}}} \;=\; \underbrace{(\bar p - Y)^2}_{B_{\text{cons}}} \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^K (p_i - \bar p)^2}_{V}. $$

Proof. Expand $(p_i - Y)^2 = \bigl((p_i - \bar p) + (\bar p - Y)\bigr)^2$:

$$ (p_i - Y)^2 = (p_i - \bar p)^2 + 2(p_i - \bar p)(\bar p - Y) + (\bar p - Y)^2. $$

Sum over $i$ and divide by $K$:

$$ \frac{1}{K}\sum_i (p_i - Y)^2 = \frac{1}{K}\sum_i (p_i - \bar p)^2 + 2(\bar p - Y)\underbrace{\frac{1}{K}\sum_i (p_i - \bar p)}_{=\,0} + (\bar p - Y)^2. $$

The middle term vanishes because $\bar p$ is the arithmetic mean. $\qquad\square$

Why it matters. This identity is the engine of everything. It decomposes per-sample Brier on a paraphrase group into (i) how far the consensus is from truth and (ii) how much the paraphrases disagree. Our reward adds an extra $\lambda V$ term to (ii), so $\lambda$ directly tunes how much we value cross-paraphrase agreement relative to consensus accuracy.

6.2 Aggregate reward closed form

The per-sample reward is $R_i = -(p_i - Y)^2 - \lambda (p_i - \bar p)^2$. Average:

$$ \frac{1}{K}\sum_i R_i = -\,\frac{1}{K}\sum_i (p_i - Y)^2 \;-\; \lambda\,\frac{1}{K}\sum_i (p_i - \bar p)^2. $$

Apply the identity in §6.1 to the first term:

$$ \frac{1}{K}\sum_i R_i = -\bigl[B_{\text{cons}} + V\bigr] - \lambda V = -\,B_{\text{cons}} \;-\; (1 + \lambda)\,V. $$

Two consequences. First, at the group level the objective is linear and strictly decreasing in $\lambda$ whenever $V > 0$ — the more wording-dependent the forecasts, the harder they get penalized as $\lambda$ grows. Second, the $\lambda = 0$ case is not variance-free: it still penalizes variance with effective weight $1$ through the bias-variance decomposition. The novelty of $\lambda > 0$ is an incremental push on top of that.

6.3 Proposition 1 — Frame-invariant Bayes optimality

Setup. Let $\mathcal P$ be a joint distribution over $(Q, Y) \in \mathcal Q \times \{0,1\}$. Let $\rho: \mathcal Q \to \Delta(\mathcal X)$ be a measurable paraphrase distribution. Let $\Pi$ be the set of measurable policies $\pi: \mathcal X \to [0,1]$. Define

$$ J_\lambda(\pi) = \mathbb E_{(Q,Y) \sim \mathcal P}\;\mathbb E_{x_{1:K} \sim \rho(\cdot\mid Q)^{\otimes K}} \!\left[\,-\frac{1}{K}\sum_i (\pi(x_i) - Y)^2 \;-\; \frac{\lambda}{K}\sum_i (\pi(x_i) - \bar p(x_{1:K}))^2\right]. $$

Proposition 1. Assume $\Pi$ contains every measurable function (sufficient capacity). Let $\pi_\lambda^\star \in \arg\max_{\pi \in \Pi} J_\lambda(\pi)$. Then

  1. Invariance in the limit. As $\lambda \to \infty$, any weak accumulation point $\pi_\infty^\star$ satisfies $\pi_\infty^\star(x) = \pi_\infty^\star(x')$ for all $x, x' \in \operatorname{supp}\rho(\cdot\mid Q)$, $\mathcal P$-almost surely in $Q$.
  2. Bayes optimality. That frame-invariant limit equals the Bayes-optimal forecaster: $\pi_\infty^\star(x) = \mathbb E_\mathcal P[Y \mid Q]$, $\mathcal P$-almost surely.

Proof sketch. Define $f_\pi(Q) = \mathbb E_{x \sim \rho(\cdot\mid Q)}[\pi(x)]$ and $\sigma^2_\pi(Q) = \mathbb E_x[(\pi(x) - f_\pi(Q))^2]$. Using the bias-variance identity (§6.1) in expectation and noting that the finite-$K$ empirical variance converges to $\sigma^2_\pi(Q)$ with an $O(1/K)$ correction:

$$ J_\lambda(\pi) = -\,\mathbb E_Q\!\Bigl[\,(f_\pi(Q) - \mathbb E[Y\mid Q])^2 \;+\; \operatorname{Var}(Y\mid Q) \;+\; (1+\lambda)\,\sigma^2_\pi(Q)\,\Bigr] + O(1/K). $$

Hold $\lambda$ fixed. Maximizing $J_\lambda$ over $\pi \in \Pi$ requires simultaneously minimizing two non-negative terms: the bias $(f_\pi(Q) - \mathbb E[Y\mid Q])^2$ and the spread $(1+\lambda)\,\sigma^2_\pi(Q)$. (The $\operatorname{Var}(Y\mid Q)$ term is an irreducible property of the data.) Sufficient capacity lets us minimize each separately.

Send $\lambda \to \infty$. The spread term dominates. Any accumulation point must satisfy $\sigma^2_{\pi_\infty^\star}(Q) = 0$ for $\mathcal P$-a.e. $Q$ — that is, $\pi_\infty^\star$ is constant on the support of $\rho(\cdot\mid Q)$. This is part (i): frame-invariance.

Conditional on frame-invariance, $\pi_\infty^\star(x) = c(Q)$ for some function $c$. The bias term becomes $(c(Q) - \mathbb E[Y\mid Q])^2$, a strictly convex function of $c(Q)$. Its unique minimizer is $c(Q) = \mathbb E[Y\mid Q]$, the Bayes-optimal forecaster. This is part (ii). $\qquad\square$

Positioning. We present this proposition as justification that the objective correctly incentivizes what we claim it incentivizes, not as novel theory. It is a corollary of (a) strict propriety of Brier (Gneiting & Raftery 2007), (b) regularized-MDP contraction (Geist, Scherrer & Pietquin 2019), and (c) the bias-variance identity. We credit these explicitly.

6.4 Hand-worked example (appendix B)

Three paraphrases, outcome $Y = 1$, forecasts $p = (0.8, 0.6, 0.7)$.

  • Consensus $\bar p = (0.8 + 0.6 + 0.7)/3 = 0.70$.
  • Paraphrase variance $V = \frac{(0.1)^2 + (-0.1)^2 + 0^2}{3} = 0.00\overline{6}$.
  • Consensus Brier $B_{\text{cons}} = (0.7 - 1)^2 = 0.09$.
  • Mean per-sample Brier $B_{\text{mean}} = \frac{0.04 + 0.16 + 0.09}{3} = 0.096\overline{6}$.
  • Identity check: $B_{\text{cons}} + V = 0.09 + 0.00\overline{6} = 0.096\overline{6} = B_{\text{mean}}$. ✓

Per-sample reward at $\lambda = 1$:

  • $R_1 = -(0.8 - 1)^2 - 1 \cdot (0.8 - 0.7)^2 = -0.04 - 0.01 = -0.05$
  • $R_2 = -(0.6 - 1)^2 - 1 \cdot (0.6 - 0.7)^2 = -0.16 - 0.01 = -0.17$
  • $R_3 = -(0.7 - 1)^2 - 1 \cdot (0.7 - 0.7)^2 = -0.09 - 0.00 = -0.09$
  • Aggregate $\tfrac{1}{3}\sum R_i = -0.103\overline{3}$.
  • Closed form $-B_{\text{cons}} - (1+\lambda)V = -0.09 - 2 \cdot 0.00\overline{6} = -0.103\overline{3}$. ✓

This is exactly what the unit tests in test_reward.py verify on 61 hand-computed cases.

7. Experimental protocol — steps to show

Every step below is required for the paper. Skipping any of them produces either an unpublishable result or one a reviewer will rightfully reject. The order matters.

7.1 Data preparation with leakage guards

  1. Pull ~4,000 resolved binary questions from Metaculus. Filter to resolution_date > 2024-06-01 and exclude anything whose resolution fell before the base-model pretraining cutoff (2024-03-01 for Qwen2.5). This is non-negotiable: a model that saw its test outcomes during pretraining will post Brier numbers that mean nothing.
  2. Split 80/10/10 train/val/test, stratified by resolution date (not by question_id — calendar-adjacent questions are too correlated).
  3. Pull the ForecastBench test slice with the same date filter.
  4. Sanity-check base rate is in $[0.2, 0.8]$; if not, investigate selection bias.
  5. Persist: data/raw/*.jsonl + *.meta.json (counts, base rate, date histogram). Any downstream run loads these.

7.2 Paraphrase generation

  1. For each training question generate $K = 8$ paraphrases with the primary paraphraser (Claude Sonnet 4.6).
  2. Every candidate passes StrictEntityDateGuard — dates, numbers, and capitalized multi-letter tokens must be preserved exactly.
  3. Every candidate then passes a bidirectional NLI entailment filter (DeBERTa-v3-MNLI, threshold 0.85 both directions).
  4. Rejected candidates are replaced; log reasons to data/paraphrased/*.rejections.jsonl.
  5. For the test set, generate two paraphrase packs: one from the primary paraphraser (in-distribution), one from the held-out paraphraser (GPT-4o-mini). This is how the OOD claim is tested.
  6. Cache all API responses on disk (api_cache/) keyed by (prompt, model, temperature, n) so reruns are free.

8. Success criteria — what results we need

The paper works if we can walk a reviewer through the following four claims, each backed by a specific table/figure and each surviving the sanity checks in §7.7.

8.1 Claim 1 — The method trains

Training loss decreases monotonically; KL to reference stays bounded; reward components ($B_{\text{cons}}$, $V$) both decrease during training. Baseline is trivial but must be stated.

What we need: a training-curve figure showing $R$, $B_{\text{cons}}$, $V$, and $\operatorname{KL}$ over steps, stable across $\geq 3$ seeds.

8.2 Claim 2 — Invariance is measurably induced

ParaSD at $\lambda > 0$ is strictly lower than at $\lambda = 0$ — on both in-distribution and held-out paraphrasers. This is the weakest claim we need to survive.

What we need: ParaSD at $\lambda = 1$ should be at least $30\%$ lower than ParaSD at $\lambda = 0$, with the OOD gap no more than $1.5\times$ the in-distribution gap. Effect size reported with seed-min and seed-max; paired-bootstrap $p < 0.05$.

8.3 Claim 3 — Accuracy is preserved or improved

This is the load-bearing empirical claim and the hardest one. Three possible outcomes, all publishable:

OutcomeWhat the numbers look likeHow we frame the paper
A. Invariance helps Brier at some $\lambda^\star > 0$ is $\geq 0.005$ lower (≈2–3% relative) than Brier at $\lambda = 0$, in-distribution, surviving seeds. OOD Brier follows the same pattern. Headline: "Paraphrase-variance penalty improves LLM forecaster accuracy." Best outcome.
B. Invariance is free Brier at $\lambda^\star$ is within $\pm 0.002$ of Brier at $\lambda = 0$, but ParaSD is materially lower. Headline: "Paraphrase-invariance is free." Useful for agentic/ensembled systems where paraphrase-robustness has separate value.
C. Invariance hurts Brier increases monotonically in $\lambda$. Headline: "The cost of paraphrase-invariance." Negative result with a clean trade-off curve; workshop explicitly welcomes these.
What we need (for the good outcome): Brier$(\lambda=1)$ in-dist beats Brier$(\lambda=0)$ by $\geq 0.003$ absolute, with a paired-bootstrap confidence interval that excludes zero. OOD Brier shows the same sign of effect.

8.4 Claim 4 — Invariance transfers OOD

The single most important diagnostic. If a model trained with a Claude paraphraser only has low ParaSD on Claude paraphrases but high ParaSD on GPT-4o-mini paraphrases, it has memorized surface patterns of one distribution rather than learned anything about semantic equivalence.

What we need: OOD ParaSD at $\lambda = 1$ must be no worse than $1.5\times$ in-dist ParaSD at the same $\lambda$. OOD Brier degradation relative to in-dist must be at most $+0.010$ absolute. If the OOD gap exceeds this, the method is primarily surface-pattern overfitting and the paper needs to say so.

8.5 Secondary claims (nice-to-have)

  • RL beats SFT-for-invariance (B5 vs B3) by $\geq 0.005$ absolute Brier.
  • RL beats post-hoc CAP projection (B5 vs B4) by $\geq 0.003$ absolute Brier on OOD paraphrases specifically.
  • Scaling ablation: effect size does not shrink monotonically with data scale (otherwise the claim is that small-data matters, not the method).
  • Model-size scaling (if compute allows): the effect transfers from 7B to 14B at qualitatively the same sign.

RoParQ-style SFT matches or beats our method SFT is a harder baseline than expected. Reposition the paper around the theoretical result + a cleaner empirical story on OOD; emphasize the capacity argument.

9. Risks & mitigations

RiskProbabilityMitigation
R1. Empirical null result. Moderate Outcome B or C above — still publishable. Frame accordingly.
R2. Degenerate invariance (constant outputs). Low–moderate KL anchor prevents collapse; sanity-check questions catch it; report both.
R3. Training-cutoff leakage. Moderate Strict date filter; leakage-stratified reporting; question-creation-date filter as additional guard.
R4. OOD invariance fails (surface-pattern overfitting). Moderate OOD Brier is a primary metric, not a footnote. If it fails, we say so and the paper becomes a cautionary tale rather than a win — still publishable.
R5. Timeline slippage past Week 2. Moderate Week 2 is a hard gate. If baselines aren’t reproducible by May 6, drop $R$/$K$ ablations and ship MVP.
R6. RoParQ extends to forecasting + RL during April/May. Low Weekly arXiv audit on authors + RoParQ forward citations. Pivot to differentiation-heavy framing if it happens.
R7. Compute failure. Low–moderate Fallback to Qwen2.5-3B (LoRA-on-4090 backup path) keeps the method story intact even if size-scaling ablation is dropped.
